ROC Curves

Receiver Operating Characteristic - Evaluating Binary Classifiers

What is a ROC Curve?

A ROC curve plots the performance of a binary classification model at all classification thresholds. It shows the trade-off between sensitivity and specificity.

X-axis: False Positive Rate (FPR)
Y-axis: True Positive Rate (TPR)
TPR = TP / (TP + FN) = Sensitivity
FPR = FP / (FP + TN) = 1 - Specificity

AUC (Area Under Curve) summarizes the entire ROC curve into a single number between 0 and 1.

The closer the curve is to the top-left corner, the better the classifier

Advantages of ROC/AUC

  • Scale-invariant: measures quality of predictions
  • Classification-threshold-invariant: measures across all thresholds
  • Easy to compare different models
  • Single number summary of performance
  • Intuitive visual representation

Limitations to Consider

  • Overly optimistic for imbalanced datasets
  • May not reflect real-world performance costs
  • Different curves can have same AUC
  • Doesn't show threshold-specific performance
  • Consider Precision-Recall for skewed data

Practical Tips for Students

When to Use ROC

Best for balanced datasets or when false positives and false negatives have similar costs. Use for screening tests, spam detection, or general classification tasks.

When to Avoid ROC

Avoid for highly imbalanced datasets (e.g., 1% positive class). Use Precision-Recall curves instead for rare disease detection or fraud detection.

Reading the Curve

Look at the curve shape, not just AUC. A curve closer to the top-left corner is better. The optimal threshold depends on your specific use case.

Model Selection

Compare multiple models by plotting their ROC curves together. The model with the highest AUC isn't always the best for your specific problem.
